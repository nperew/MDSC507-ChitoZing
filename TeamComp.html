Comparison to Past iGEM Teams



The 2017 ChiTUcare iGEM team from UT Darmstadt tackled the problem of pathogenic infections with a similar approach as ChitoZing. ChiTUcare aimed to manufacture a chitosan hydrogel that would not only aid in healing, but also allow for easy detection of bacteria in wounds.


To introduce antimicrobial and wound-healing properties to their hydrogel, ChiTUcare used chitosan as their hydrogel substrate. They mainly obtained chitin, precursor for chitosan, from marine sources such as crab shells, which were then chemically processed to create chitosan. They found that the biological activities of chitosan differed depending on its chemical properties. They saw increasing antimicrobial properties with decreasing chain length, while scar-free wound healing properties depended on the deacetylation pattern of chitosan. To enable precise production of defined chain length and deacetylation pattern oligomers, they produced chitosan in E. coli using chitin synthase, chitin deacetylases and chitinase. This ensured that they produced the optimum chitosan substrate for the production of their hydrogels.

In order to detect bacteria, ChiTUcare team used the increase in protease levels as an indicator of bacterial invasion. Specifically, alpha-chymotrypsin protease secreted by Staphylococcus aureus and Pseudomonas aeruginosa was used as the marker. To detect the presence of protease, chitosan was first modified at the amino group with succinic anhydride which provided a binding domain for a peptide that contained the fluorogenic substrate of phenylalanine. More peptides were cleaved with increased protease activity which emitted intense light, giving an easy read-out of the infection status.
