INTEGRATED HUMAN PRACTICES



Importance of Outreach:

A critical part of project development is consulting with and receiving feedback from experts and professionals in the subject area of the proposed project. It gives us the opportunity to gain insight into the strengths and shortcomings of our project design, scientific feasibility and the impact our project will have in the community.

Contacts:

To get a better understanding of our project and identify areas of improvement, we reached out to a wide range of professions including professors, dermatologists, physicians, nurses and patients.
Dr. Laurie Parsons is a dermatologist and the medical director at the Sheldon Chumir Wound Care Clinic and works alongside Alberta Health Services. She also conducts research in the Department of Cumming School of Medicine at the University of Calgary. We reached out to Dr. Parsons to ask about the feasibility of our project and how we could address allergic reactions to chitosan.
Our conversation with Dr. Parsons yielded several key considerations. First, she encouraged us to consider the cost associated with using chitosan. She mentioned that in the past, chitosan was used to suspend epidermal cells which were used as a grafting material. They saw positive results, chitosan aiding in the shift of chronic wounds to acute wounds. However, the production of chitosan-based products soon became too expensive and was discontinued by the Alberta Health Services. Dermagraft costs around $900 USD, $600 USD per becaplermin gel tube and $600 USD for allograft. Her story put a lot of emphasis on the importance of finding a cheaper alternative when creating our product.‚Äù
Secondly, she informed us that the only way to effectively address allergic reactions to chitosan was through patch testing as it is often really difficult to be predictive about allergies. Her experience working with chitosan, derived from shellfish, did not show any significant allergic reactions in patients and minimal reactions in patients allergic to seafood. She suggested we build on the work of others by finding sufficient data that show minimal allergic reactions to chitosan in patients and put an allergy warning on the package for disclosure.

Dr. Keeyoung Kim is an associate professor in the department of Mechanical and Manufacturing Engineering, Schulich School of Engineering. We spoke to Dr. Kim about various topics, including crosslinking, hydrogels, bioprinting and scaling-up of projects.
ChitoZing, being interested in creating a hydrogel wound dressing, sought advice from Dr. Kim with research experience in infections, inflammation and chronic diseases. Similar to Dr. Parsons, Dr. Kim commented on the importance of minimizing costs. He suggested using gelatin, a cheap, easy to handle/storage biopolymer to make hydrogels. A drawback to this however, would be the loss of antimicrobial properties provided by chitosan. Regardless, his suggestion was valuable as it opened up a different route ChitoZing could take with the project.
Dr. Kim also mentioned a key benefit of creating a hydrogel instead of leaving our co-polymerized chitosan and 6-gingerol product in powder form. He confirmed that durability is significantly increased when topical agents are made into hydrogels, taking us a step closer to achieving a low-maintenance topical agent. Lastly, Dr. Kim gave insight into important considerations when working to scale-up hydrogels products. Producing a patch often requires more time and equipment to produce, cut and package, thus a liquid or powder form is ideal for scaling-up. This sparked the idea of creating a user-friendly kit that includes the chitosan 6-gingerol powder and an aqueous mix-in solution as our final product.

Dr. Elmar J. Prenner is a professor in the department of Biochemistry, Faculty of Science, with broad research interests in biomembrane architecture (lipid rafts, domains), lipid-protein interactions, nanoparticle based pulmonary drug delivery; bioinorganic chemistry (membrane interactions of heavy metals); bioanalytical chemistry (bioassay development), biophysical techniques.
Discussing the project with Dr. Prenner and his masters student, Travis Issler, we were able to confirm the advantages and disadvantages of using synthetic biology to promote chitosan crosslinking with 6-gingerol and synthesis.
A large part of confirming our protocol included the assessment of alternative strategies to genuinely develop a project that relied heavily on synthetic biology as opposed to other methods. We discussed their protocol, which includes using crosslinking reagents such as glutaraldehyde to develop the crosslinked chitosan which is purified in hydrogel form.

Upon further contextualization and input from Dr. Omid Haji-Ghassemi, a professor in the department of Biochemistry, Faculty of Science, and a structural biologist, we deduced that most cross-linking agents, such as glutaraldehyde, would prove too toxic to use in the synthesis of medical equipment. They offered insight in the linker process that would help us avoid using excess chemicals and reagents to create a product that would use as few materials as possible. Dr. Haji-Ghassemi also noted that external cross-linking was a highly random process, oftentimes introducing unequal dispersion of the cross-linked molecules, which would contribute to the toxicity of the 6-gingerol if exposed in high concentrations.

The Rotary Flames House is a palliative care centre for children and a facility that first introduced one of our group members to open MRSA wounds, which is where the idea for this project came to fruition. As we continued to evolve and expand the project, we checked regularly with the palliative care nurses, inquiring about ease of application, the best texture to work with, clean-up, and other aspects of open wound care.

Sydney Potter is an occupational therapy master‚Äôs student in her final year. Occupational Therapists (OT) work with patients to help get them back to doing routine, everyday tasks. OTs offer a unique perspective on what patients may struggle with, and have an intimate knowledge of their needs. Our conversation with Sydney brought up an important issue in wound care - wound management. Chronic wounds, especially pressure sores, require repositioning every 2-4 hours. However that is an unrealistic workload for either nurses or OTs to manage and in the chaos of their day to day patients are not repositioned as often as they should be. Her suggestion was to provide some indication that a dressing needs to be adjusted, which is something no other bandage offers and would help serve as a reminder for caregivers working with patients.

Conclusion

We concluded over time that a powder form, which could be easily mixed into a soluble paste that hardens in a short period of time, would be the ideal product for storage. Because of the busy nature of such care centres, and home-care, as well, we also concluded that having the product come in the form of a kit, with pre-measured containers and solution, would make it accessible and easy to use. The idea of colour-changing materials came into play when we were discussing the periodic change of the wound packing, as well as the ease with which it could be removed.
We were also told that a big part of wound dressings is the take-off process, especially when it comes to young patients. After some literature review, we concluded that 6-gingerol, which is a polar substance, would help to solubilize the chitosan in water, granting easy removal by washing away the wound packing. The concepts surrounding reusing the packing are still evolving.
